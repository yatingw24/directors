Can Women Make Hollywood Blockbusters?

Females can be renowned filmmakers only if they stay in their lanes

By Yating Wang

We are in a golden era for women in film. Or so it seems. Greta Gerwig’s Barbie smashed box office records. Coralie Fargeat’s The Substance left Cannes dripping in acclaim. Studios boast about “historic opportunities” for female filmmakers.

Is it really progress? An unprecedented women’s victory? Or just a history repeating itself?

When only a few among multiple nominations turned into actual Oscar trophies, the institutional biases against female filmmakers become undeniable. Women aren’t being handed the keys to Hollywood but rather they’re just being allowed into certain rooms.

Critics have begun to recognize female filmmakers and their work in recent years, but often in carefully subcategories. Lists like BBC’s “The 100 greatest films directed by women” now proliferate, yet when critics are asked for a broader ranking, they are not bothered to take female’s work into consideration. In the list called “The 21st Century’s 100 Greatest Films” curated by BBC, less than 10 films are directed by females.

A recent study by the University of Southern California Annenberg Inclusion Initiative underscores this exclusion: among all distinct directors who worked from 2007 to 2024, only 10 percent are female filmmakers.

The gender gap in critical reception is stark. Among best films selected by BBC, a film directed by women receives 12 awards and 15 nominations on average, while those by men earn four times as many nominations and almost five times as many awards.

The number of awards and nominations hardly explains viewers' ratings. “Male directed fare was not evaluated any more or less favorably than female directed fare,” wrote Dr. Stacy Smith, the director of the Annenberg Inclusion Initiative. Though top films by women average 7.3 on IMDb, compared to 7.8 for films by men, no statistically significant correlation has been found between the number of awards and the rating.

Gender is a significant factor in the types of stories told by directors, as revealed in a study by Sundance Institute and Women In Film Los Angeles. Despite genres represented fairly equal in both genders’ filmwork, the top three genres tagged within well-recognized films made by men are action, thriller, and sci-fi films. For women directors - they are comedy, documentary and coming-of-age.

Male filmmakers do not necessarily direct fewer romantic comedies, explained by Patricia White, the professor of Film and Media Studies at Swarthmore College, but they are far less likely to be defined by them. But when a superwoman movie comes out, the feminine theme becomes a defining marketing sale point. What women can be entrusted is still a calculated effort in Hollywood, as Professor White noted, they can helm searing dramas, feminist horror, or coming-of-age tales, but without a gendered angle women are rarely “being entrusted in enormous tenpole movies.”

While men move fluidly between genres - from superheroes franchises to Oscar dramas - women directors often find themselves pigeonholed, their careers constrained by industry assumptions about what kinds of stories they should tell.

In terms of box office, female-directed films earn a significantly less portion compared to male-directed films. Male directors dominated the highest revenue brackets, with their films frequently reaching the 8 to 10 million range, while female directors’ work barely reached 9 million.

Women are celebrated when they conform to expectations. Until women can fail as freely as men - flopping at the box office, pivoting between genres, or directing a $200 million bomb without derailing their careers - the golden era remains a gilded cage. The system isn't just slow to change; it's designed to keep women in their place. And for now, that place is still narrowly defined.

Methodology

My data analysis investigates gender disparities in film through two key approaches: comparing critical reception and box office performance between female- and male-directed films, and examining how these gaps have evolved over the past two decades.

To assess the impact of extreme weather on the events For the first part, to compare the critical reception and performance of female-directed films versus male-directed films, I constructed a dataset from two primary lists from BBC: “The 100 greatest films directed by women” and "100 greatest films of the 21st century" which predominantly features male directors. Using BeautifulSoup, I extracted titles, directors, and publication years from these lists. I then merged this data with IMDb’s film metadata, collecting hyperlinks, ratings, award wins, nominations, and genre classifications for each film.

To assess disparities in reception, I ran a linear regression analysis to determine whether differences in ratings and awards between female- and male-directed films were statistically significant. This approach allowed us to isolate the impact of director gender on critical and institutional recognition while accounting for confounding variables. For genre analysis, I first assigned each list with the specific gender it is associated with, and then mapped IMDb’s granular genre tags into broader categories (e.g., grouping “cyberpunk” or “space” into Sci-Fi) to understand the share of certain genres by gender. The first chart in my story page is a comparison between the share of genres within work by female vs. male directors.

Next, I merge my two lists of BBC films with Box Office Mojo, a database that contains all financial information for film titles. The worldwide gross box office is extracted given the list features not only U.S. but international filmwork. Instead of complicated calculation, I represented the data in a bee swarm chart, resembling a distribution and emphasizing both the concentration of female-directed films at lower revenue tiers and the outliers.
